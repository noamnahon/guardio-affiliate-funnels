How I Almost Lost $8,000 to a Scam That Looked Completely Real

I've been online for over 20 years. I thought I knew all the tricks. I was wrong.

LM
Linda M. 58, Retired Teacher from Ohio
January 3, 2025 • 3 min read

Last month, I came within seconds of handing over my Amazon login, my credit card information, and potentially my entire savings to criminals. And the scary part? The email looked exactly like every other Amazon email I've ever received.

I'm sharing this story because I don't want anyone else to go through what I did. If it can happen to me — someone who's been online since the dial-up days — it can happen to anyone.

It Started With an Email

It was a Tuesday afternoon. I was checking my email when I saw a message from "Amazon" with the subject line: "Order Confirmation: MacBook Pro - $1,847.99"

My heart dropped. I hadn't ordered a MacBook. Someone must have gotten into my account.

The email looked perfect — the Amazon logo, the orange buttons, the formatting. Everything. At the bottom, there was a helpful link: "Didn't make this purchase? Cancel order here."

Of course I clicked it. Wouldn't you?

The Page That Almost Fooled Me

I landed on what looked exactly like Amazon's login page. Same colors, same layout, same "Sign in" button. I was about to type in my password when something made me pause.

The URL bar.

Instead of amazon.com, it said something like amazon-secure-verify.com.

I closed the tab immediately. My hands were shaking.

"I was one click away from giving criminals access to my Amazon account, my saved credit cards, and who knows what else."

The Wake-Up Call

That night, I couldn't sleep. I kept thinking: What if I hadn't noticed the URL?

I called my son the next morning. He's in IT, and I told him what happened. His response surprised me.

"Mom, you got lucky. Those phishing sites are getting so good that even I almost fell for one last year."

He helped me check if my email had been in any data breaches. Turns out, my email address was exposed in three different breaches over the past few years — and I had no idea.

That explained how they knew my name and that I shop on Amazon. They weren't randomly targeting me. They knew things about me.

What I Did Next

My son recommended I install something on my browser that would catch these fake sites before I could even see them. He said it works in the background — I wouldn't have to do anything.

I was skeptical. I've tried antivirus software before and it always slowed down my computer or bombarded me with popups.

But I trusted him, so I tried it.

A week later, I got another suspicious email. This time, when I clicked the link (old habits die hard), a warning popped up immediately: "This site has been identified as a phishing attempt."

It blocked me from even seeing the fake page.

That's when I realized: I don't have to be a computer expert to stay safe online. I just need the right tool watching my back.

Why I'm Sharing This

I'm not a tech person. I'm a retired teacher who likes to shop online, check Facebook, and email my grandkids. I shouldn't have to worry about criminals every time I open my inbox.

If you're like me — someone who uses the internet but doesn't want to become a cybersecurity expert — I want you to know: you're not alone, and there is help.

The tool my son recommended has been running quietly on my browser for a month now. I've completely forgotten it's there — until it blocks something dangerous. And that's exactly how I want it.

I'm not going to tell you what to do. But if my story sounds familiar, or if you've ever wondered "am I protected?" — it might be worth taking a look.
